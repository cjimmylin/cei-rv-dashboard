Overview — Systematic Screening Pipeline
Systematic screening of 401 religious AI benchmarks yielded 47 gate-passers — an 88.3% attrition rate reflecting how few benchmarks engage religion substantively.
We applied a systematic screening pipeline to 401 candidate religious AI benchmarks identified through database search (Semantic Scholar, arXiv, Google Scholar), citation chaining, and expert referral. Each candidate received a score on a six-component Religious Discriminability (RD) rubric measuring depth of engagement with religious content. Scores range from incidental mention (near 0) to substantive theological evaluation (near 100). Candidates passed both hard gates — RD ≥ 30 and publicly accessible evaluation data (GitHub, HuggingFace, or equivalent) — to enter the analytical sample. The 88.3% attrition rate confirms that most candidates touch religion peripherally.

What you will learn from this tab: (1) How many benchmarks survive quality screening, (2) which religious traditions dominate, (3) which tradition families are completely absent, and (4) why the distribution reflects infrastructure, not theological importance. The four stat cards show headline numbers; two charts disaggregate gate-passers by tradition and tradition family.

Sensitivity Analysis — Robustness of Rankings and Findings
Three sensitivity analyses test whether the paper's findings survive alternative analytical choices: bootstrap resampling, weight specification, and gate threshold variation.
(1) How stable are individual benchmark rankings? Bootstrap resampling (10,000 Dirichlet-weighted iterations, i.e., random weight sampling) quantifies uncertainty in composite scores and top-15 membership.
(2) Do rankings change if we weight dimensions differently? Weight specification sensitivity tests 61 alternative weight vectors across the six rubric dimensions.
(3) Is the RD ≥ 30 gate defensible? Gate threshold sensitivity examines how pool size and tradition coverage change if the gate is raised or lowered.

Together, these analyses reveal where the paper's conclusions are robust and where intellectual humility is warranted. Sensitivity analysis is the primary instrument for separating findings that survive alternative choices from those that are parameter artifacts.

Tradition Void — Why Eight Traditions Have No Viable Benchmarks
Eight of 14 traditions have zero viable benchmarks. This tab identifies the structural factors — digital corpus, NLP community, formalizability, data sovereignty, institutional support, publication infrastructure — that explain the void.
The "tradition void" is a structured pattern of exclusion driven by identifiable infrastructure gaps. We score each tradition on six structural factors (each 0-5):

• Digital Corpus — availability of machine-readable religious texts
• NLP Community — size and activity of computational linguistics researchers
• Formalizability — degree to which doctrine can be structured into testable propositions
• Data Sovereignty — whether knowledge-sharing norms permit public dataset creation
• Institutional Support — organizations bridging religious scholarship and computational research
• Publication Infrastructure — journals, shared tasks, workshops, and repositories for dissemination

The analysis tests whether the void reflects inherent tradition properties (oral transmission, lack of formal doctrine) or research ecosystem gaps (absent computational religious studies communities).

Values Relevance — Do Religious Benchmarks Actually Measure Values?
Most "religious" benchmarks test factual knowledge, not values reasoning. The A3 score (0-100) measures values alignment; the A1 classification identifies what each benchmark actually measures.
A benchmark can be "religious" in subject matter without measuring religious values. Identifying Quranic verses differs fundamentally from reasoning within a religious ethical framework on a novel moral dilemma. This tab reports two complementary assessments:

A3 Values Alignment Relevance (0-100): The theological advisory council assessed each benchmark's items, constructs, and response format. Scores of 0-25 indicate factual/knowledge testing; 25-50, some values engagement; 50-75, meaningful values reasoning; 75-100, deep engagement with tradition-specific ethical frameworks.

A1 Construct Type Classification: What each benchmark primarily measures — knowledge/factual, sentiment/attitude, bias/fairness, theological reasoning, values/ethics, or mixed. This reveals what benchmarks actually evaluate versus what their names imply.

A benchmark labeled "Islamic Ethics Evaluation" that only asks factual questions about the Five Pillars measures religious literacy, not ethics. The A3 score distinguishes these constructs.

Cross-Vendor LLM Analysis — How Do Benchmarks Perform Across Model Families?
The construct validity battery (65 items) and T2 Arabic MCQ (30 items) were administered to 9 LLMs across 3 vendors, revealing three distinct behavioral modes.
A 12-member expert council analyzed how these instruments perform across 9 models in 3 vendor families: Anthropic (Opus, Sonnet, Haiku), OpenAI (GPT-Codex, Codex-Mini), and Google (Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite, 2.5 Flash, 2.5 Pro, 3.1 Pro). Three behavioral modes emerged. Anthropic models produce elaborated, gradient-sensitive responses with near-perfect rubric scores. OpenAI models produce terse but accurate responses. Gemini models produce flat, template-length responses scoring 0.0 on knowledge/reasoning/values rubrics. These divergent patterns challenge whether religious values benchmarks can claim cross-vendor validity.

The Gemini r = 1.0 values are degenerate: near-zero variance in both measures produces a perfect correlation by mathematical default.
Three key findings emerge from cross-vendor analysis. First, rubric-based evaluation has a fundamental ceiling-floor problem: it cannot separate frontier models (all ≥ 0.93) or explain Gemini's failure mode (all 0.0 on K/R/V). Second, the T2 Arabic MCQ provides the widest cross-vendor performance spread — 93.3 percentage points from 6.7% to 100%. Domain-specific, non-English, reasoning-intensive MCQ items may define the future of religious values benchmarking. Third, the K < R < V response length gradient is vendor-specific: it holds across all Anthropic models, partially in OpenAI, and is absent in Gemini. This pattern requires investigation across additional model families before adoption as a construct validity indicator.
Tab conclusion — Council consensus: Cross-vendor replication fundamentally changes the paper's contribution. The K < R < V gradient, rubric ceiling, and bias-values independence are confirmed within the Claude family and partially replicated in Codex models. Gemini's complete failure raises critical methodological questions. The council reaches three consensus recommendations: (1) No religious values benchmark should be published without multi-vendor validation on ≥3 model families. (2) The field should adopt hybrid evaluation architectures combining MCQ accuracy floors with response-length engagement metrics. Rubric-only approaches suffer ceiling-floor effects that collapse model tiers. (3) The T2 Arabic MCQ finding — that domain-specific, non-English, reasoning-intensive items produce the widest cross-vendor performance spread — should guide next-generation benchmark design. Gemini's template-like responses may reflect safety filtering, prompt incompatibility, or capability gaps. Replication with verified API access is essential before drawing causal conclusions. The gradient's absence in Gemini limits universality claims until additional model families (Llama, Mistral) are tested.

Methodology

401 benchmark candidates were identified through Semantic Scholar, arXiv, Google Scholar, citation chaining, and expert referral. Each was scored on a 6-component Religious Discriminability rubric. 47 passed both hard gates (RD ≥ 30 and public data). Rankings use a 3-axis composite (Religious Depth 40%, Practical Ease 35%, Citation Impact 25%) with bootstrap uncertainty quantification. Cross-vendor LLM replication used 65 construct validity items and 30 Arabic MCQ items across 9 models.
