Religious Values Benchmark: Cross-Vendor Meta-Evaluation

9 Models × 3 Vendors | Dashboard v4 | 2026-04-05 | 12-Member Expert Council Review
This dashboard presents a systematic meta-evaluation of 401 religious AI benchmark candidates across 14 religious traditions, validated across 9 large language models from 3 vendor families (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google Gemini). Of 401 candidates, 47 pass quality gates. Cross-vendor replication reveals three distinct behavioral modes: Anthropic models produce elaborated responses with near-perfect rubric scores, OpenAI models produce terse but accurate responses, and Gemini models produce template-flat responses scoring near zero on content rubrics. The benchmark ecosystem is dominated by Islam (59.6% of gate-passers) due to Arabic NLP infrastructure; 8 traditions have zero viable benchmarks; most benchmarks test factual recall, not values reasoning (mean A3 = 33.6/100); and the only instrument that discriminates across vendors is a domain-specific Arabic MCQ (6.7%–100% accuracy range). Overview — Systematic Screening Pipeline
This tab reports the outcome of a systematic screening pipeline applied to 401 candidate religious AI benchmarks identified through structured database search (Semantic Scholar, arXiv, Google Scholar), forward/backward citation chaining, and expert referral. Each candidate was scored on a six-component Religious Discriminability (RD) rubric that measures the depth of engagement with religious content — ranging from incidental mention (score near 0) to substantive theological evaluation (score near 100). Only candidates achieving RD ≥ 30 and providing publicly accessible evaluation data (GitHub, HuggingFace, or equivalent) passed both hard gates and entered the analytical sample. The 88.3% attrition rate — from 401 screened to 47 gate-passers — reflects the stringency of these criteria: most candidates touch religion peripherally rather than measuring religious constructs as their primary evaluation target.

What you will learn from this tab: (1) How many benchmarks survive quality screening, (2) which religious traditions dominate the benchmark landscape, (3) which tradition families are completely absent, and (4) why the distribution reflects infrastructure, not theological importance. The four stat cards show headline numbers; the two charts below disaggregate gate-passers by individual tradition and by tradition family.

Sensitivity Analysis — Robustness of Rankings and Findings
A central methodological obligation in benchmark meta-evaluation is transparency about how sensitive findings are to specific analytical choices. This tab presents three complementary sensitivity analyses that answer three critical questions:

(1) How stable are individual benchmark rankings? Bootstrap resampling (10,000 Dirichlet-weighted iterations) quantifies uncertainty in composite scores and top-15 membership.
(2) Do rankings change if we weight dimensions differently? Weight specification sensitivity tests 61 alternative weight vectors across the six rubric dimensions.
(3) Is the RD ≥ 30 gate defensible? Gate threshold sensitivity examines how pool size and tradition coverage change if the gate is raised or lowered.

Taken together, these analyses reveal where the paper's conclusions are robust and where intellectual humility is warranted. From a psychometric standpoint, sensitivity analysis is not optional in meta-evaluation — it is the primary instrument for distinguishing findings that would survive alternative analytical choices from those that are artifacts of specific parameter settings.

How to read this forest plot: Each horizontal bar represents one gate-passing benchmark. The center point is its mean composite score across 10,000 bootstrap resamples; the whiskers span the 95% confidence interval. The red dashed vertical line marks the top-15 cutoff score. Benchmarks whose confidence intervals cross this line have uncertain top-15 membership — their ranking could plausibly place them inside or outside the top 15 depending on which sample of weights is drawn. The average CI width is 15.87 points on a 100-point composite scale. Of 47 gate-passers: 9 are certain top-15 members (CI entirely above cutoff), 6 are likely (mean above cutoff, CI overlaps), and 8 are contested (CI straddles the cutoff). The remaining 24 are clearly outside the top 15. Treat ordinal rank differences between benchmarks near the cutoff with caution — the statistical uncertainty is substantial.

Interpretation: The average CI width of 15.87 points exceeds the composite score spread between adjacent benchmarks, meaning most pairwise rank differences are not statistically distinguishable. The tiered approach (Certain/Likely/Contested) is methodologically superior to ordinal ranking for this reason. For practitioners selecting benchmarks, the "Certain top-15" group of 9 benchmarks is the only set whose membership is robust to analytical variation. The remaining 6 "Likely" benchmarks should be treated as provisional recommendations, not definitive selections.

Tradition Void — Why Eight Traditions Have No Viable Benchmarks
The "tradition void" is not merely an absence of data — it is a structured pattern of exclusion driven by specific, identifiable infrastructure gaps. This tab investigates why 8 of 14 major religious traditions have zero gate-passing benchmarks by scoring each tradition on six structural factors (each scored 0-5):

• Digital Corpus — Availability of machine-readable religious texts (scriptures, commentaries, legal rulings) in digitized form
• NLP Community — Size and activity of computational linguistics researchers working on the tradition's languages and texts
• Formalizability — Degree to which doctrinal content can be structured into testable propositions (e.g., legal rulings, logical arguments, ethical principles)
• Data Sovereignty — Whether knowledge-sharing norms permit public dataset creation (some traditions restrict sacred knowledge dissemination)
• Institutional Support — Existence of universities, centers, or organizations bridging religious scholarship and computational research
• Publication Infrastructure — Availability of journals, shared tasks, workshops, and repositories (GitHub, HuggingFace) for benchmark dissemination

The analysis tests whether the void can be explained by inherent properties of the traditions (e.g., oral transmission, lack of formal doctrine) or by research ecosystem gaps (e.g., absence of computational religious studies communities).

Indigenous Spirituality's uniformly low profile is not a measure of its intellectual sophistication — traditions with millennia of accumulated wisdom score low because their knowledge systems are oral, place-based, and governed by custodial protocols (CARE Principles) that rightly restrict public dataset creation.

Values Relevance — Do Religious Benchmarks Actually Measure Values?
A benchmark can be "religious" in subject matter without measuring anything about religious values. Knowing that a model can identify Quranic verses or name Buddhist sects is different from knowing whether it can reason within a religious ethical framework when confronted with a novel moral dilemma. This tab reports two complementary assessments:

A3 Values Alignment Relevance (0-100): A rubric assessing whether each benchmark tests value-laden reasoning (high A3) versus factual knowledge recall or bias detection (low A3). Scores were assigned through structured assessment of each benchmark's items, constructs, and response format by the theological advisory council. A score of 0-25 indicates primarily factual/knowledge testing; 25-50 indicates some values engagement; 50-75 indicates meaningful values reasoning; 75-100 indicates deep engagement with tradition-specific ethical frameworks.

A1 Construct Type Classification: What each benchmark primarily measures, categorized as knowledge/factual, sentiment/attitude, bias/fairness, theological reasoning, values/ethics, or mixed. This classification reveals what the field's benchmarks actually evaluate versus what their names imply.

The central concern is this: a benchmark labeled "Islamic Ethics Evaluation" that only asks factual questions about the Five Pillars is not measuring ethics — it is measuring religious literacy. The A3 score distinguishes these fundamentally different constructs.

A benchmark scoring below A3 = 50 is testing the latter, regardless of how it is marketed. The dominance of knowledge/factual benchmarks in the A1 pie chart reveals a field-wide construct validity failure: these instruments measure what is easy to test (factual recall), not what matters for deployment (values reasoning).

Claim Evidence — Pre-Registered Hypotheses and Statistical Tests
This tab presents the evidentiary record for each of the paper's seven central claims. The claims are:

C1: Tradition Asymmetry — Islam dominates the benchmark ecosystem
C2: Knowledge-Reasoning Conflation — most benchmarks test knowledge, not reasoning
C3: Data Availability Bottleneck — 42.4% of candidates lack public data
C4: Publication Infrastructure Barrier — infrastructure, not formalizability, predicts benchmarks
C5: Field Recency — 73% of candidates are from 2024-2026
C6: WEIRD Bias Reversal — non-WEIRD traditions pass gates at higher rates
C7: Bias-Values Conflation — benchmarks conflate bias detection with values alignment

Each claim was tested using the appropriate inferential procedure: chi-square tests for distributional asymmetry, binomial tests for proportion hypotheses, Spearman correlations for structural predictors, logistic regression for multivariate prediction, and bootstrap tests for ranking stability. Tests were specified before analysis; the results — including null results — are reported with equal prominence. Transparent reporting of unsupported claims is as important to the paper's credibility as the supported ones — the field does not benefit from selective presentation.

Three key findings emerge from cross-vendor analysis. First, rubric-based evaluation has a fundamental ceiling-floor problem: it cannot discriminate between frontier models (all scoring ≥ 0.93) or explain Gemini’s failure mode (all scoring 0.0 on K/R/V). Second, the T2 Arabic MCQ is the only benchmark component that provides genuine cross-vendor discrimination, with a 93.3 percentage-point spread from 6.7% to 100%. This suggests that domain-specific, non-English, reasoning-intensive MCQ items may be the future of religious values benchmarking. Third, the K < R < V response length gradient is vendor-specific rather than universal: it holds across all Anthropic models, partially in OpenAI, and is absent in Gemini. This pattern warrants investigation across additional model families before being adopted as a construct validity indicator. Multi-vendor validation is essential before any benchmark can claim to measure religious values competency.
Tab conclusion — Council consensus: Cross-vendor replication fundamentally changes the paper's contribution. The K < R < V gradient, rubric ceiling, and bias-values independence findings are confirmed within the Claude family and partially replicated in Codex models, but Gemini's complete failure raises critical methodological questions. The council reaches three consensus recommendations: (1) No religious values benchmark should be published without multi-vendor validation on ≥3 model families. (2) The field should adopt hybrid evaluation architectures combining MCQ accuracy floors with response-length engagement metrics, since rubric-only approaches suffer from ceiling-floor effects that cannot discriminate model tiers. (3) The T2 Arabic MCQ finding — that domain-specific, non-English, reasoning-intensive items provide the only genuine cross-vendor discrimination — should guide the next generation of benchmark design. The Gemini results must be interpreted cautiously — template-like responses may reflect safety filtering, prompt incompatibility, or capability gaps, and replication with verified API access is essential before drawing causal conclusions. The gradient's absence in Gemini does not invalidate it as a measurement construct, but it does limit universality claims until non-Claude, non-Gemini models (Llama, Mistral) are tested.

Methodology

401 benchmark candidates were identified through Semantic Scholar, arXiv, Google Scholar, citation chaining, and expert referral. Each was scored on a 6-component Religious Discriminability rubric. 47 passed both hard gates (RD ≥ 30 and public data). Rankings use a 3-axis composite (Religious Depth 40%, Practical Ease 35%, Citation Impact 25%) with bootstrap uncertainty quantification. Cross-vendor LLM replication used 65 construct validity items and 30 Arabic MCQ items across 9 models.
